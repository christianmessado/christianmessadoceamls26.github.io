what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on further developing and understanding the Step 6 model training pipeline for our post-disaster building damage assessment project. We worked through the different components required to train a deep learning model, including model architecture selection, loss function configuration, optimization strategies, and experiment tracking.

  One of the most valuable things I learned was how each part of the training pipeline contributes to model performance. The U-Net architecture serves as the foundation for semantic segmentation by predicting damage classifications at the pixel level. We also implemented a weighted CrossEntropy loss function to address class imbalance within the dataset, ensuring that underrepresented damage categories receive greater importance during training.

  I gained a better understanding of how optimizers update model parameters during training and how learning rate schedulers help improve convergence by adjusting the learning rate throughout the training process. Additionally, we integrated Weights & Biases (W&B) to track experiments, visualize metrics, and compare model performance across multiple training runs.

  Beyond implementation, I spent time reviewing and explaining the code itself, which helped reinforce my understanding of how data moves through the pipeline from loading and preprocessing to prediction, loss calculation, backpropagation, and evaluation.

blockers: |
  One challenge today was debugging issues related to dataset loading and file paths. Since the training pipeline depends on correctly locating image patches and corresponding labels, even small directory mismatches can prevent the model from training successfully.

  Another challenge was understanding how the various training components interact with one another. While concepts such as loss functions, optimizers, and schedulers are straightforward individually, seeing how they work together in a complete training workflow required additional study and experimentation.

reflection: |
  Today's work helped bridge the gap between the theoretical concepts we have been learning through research papers and their practical implementation in code. Reading about neural networks and computer vision architectures is valuable, but building and analyzing a complete training pipeline provided a much deeper understanding of how these systems operate in real-world applications.

  Looking back at the progress our team has made, it is exciting to see our project moving from literature review and methodology development into actual model training. Each step brings us closer to evaluating our research questions and determining which architectures perform best for post-disaster building damage assessment. I am looking forward to running experiments, analyzing results, and continuing to improve our models in the coming weeks.

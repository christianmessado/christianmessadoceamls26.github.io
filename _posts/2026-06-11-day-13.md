what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on working directly with the xView2 dataset and learning how satellite imagery is prepared for computer vision research. We explored GeoTIFF files,        which contain the pre-disaster and post-disaster satellite images used in our project. I learned how these files differ from traditional image formats and how specialized   tools such as Rasterio can be used to read and process them.

  One of the main tasks was creating a function that loads GeoTIFF imagery, extracts the RGB image bands, and normalizes the pixel values so the images can be displayed and   analyzed correctly. After processing a sample image, we were able to visualize pre-disaster satellite imagery and better understand the structure of the data we will be     working with throughout the project.

  We also began building image pairs from the xView2 dataset. This involved identifying matching pre-disaster and post-disaster images, locating their corresponding           annotation files, and organizing them into a structured dataset index. This step is important because it creates the relationship between the before-and-after images that   will later be used for building damage assessment and change detection.

blockers: |
  No major blockers today. The biggest challenge was understanding the structure of GeoTIFF imagery and how satellite image data is stored compared to traditional image       formats. However, working through the preprocessing steps helped make the concepts much clearer.

reflection: |
  Today's work gave me a much deeper appreciation for the amount of preparation required before machine learning models can be trained. While research papers often focus on   model architecture and performance, I am beginning to see how critical data organization and preprocessing are to the success of a project. Learning how to process          GeoTIFF imagery and build image pairs helped me better understand the foundation of our dataset and how it will support the later stages of model training and evaluation.   It was exciting to move beyond planning and begin working directly with the data itself.

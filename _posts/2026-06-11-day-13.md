what_i_learned: |

  Today focused on preparing the xView2 dataset for future model development. I learned how GeoTIFF satellite imagery differs from traditional image formats and how specialized tools such as Rasterio are used to read and process this type of data. We explored the structure of GeoTIFF files, including image bands, pixel values, and metadata, and learned how to convert the imagery into a format that can be visualized and analyzed using Python.

  One of the most important tasks was creating a reusable function to read and normalize GeoTIFF images. This involved loading specific image bands, rearranging image dimensions, and scaling pixel values so the images could be displayed properly. After verifying that the images loaded correctly, we visualized pre-disaster satellite imagery and examined its characteristics.

  We also worked on building image pairs from the xView2 dataset. This process involved identifying matching pre-disaster and post-disaster images, locating their corresponding annotation files, and organizing everything into a structured dataset index. By creating these image pairs, we established the foundation needed for future building damage assessment and change detection tasks.

blockers: |

  No major blockers today. Some of the concepts related to GeoTIFF imagery and satellite data were new to me, but breaking the process into smaller steps made it much easier to understand how the data is prepared before model training can begin.

reflection: |

  Today gave me a much better understanding of the data preparation side of computer vision research. Before this project, I mainly thought about machine learning models, but I am beginning to see how much work goes into organizing and preprocessing data before a model can even be trained. Building image pairs and learning how satellite imagery is handled helped me better understand the overall workflow of our project. Each step brings us closer to developing a system capable of assessing post-disaster building damage using AI.

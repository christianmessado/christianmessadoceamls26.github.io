what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on completing Step 6 of our methodology pipeline: training the baseline model for post-disaster building damage assessment. This marked an important transition in our project, as we moved from research, literature reviews, and dataset preparation into the model development stage.

  One of the main tasks was implementing a U-Net model with a ResNet50 encoder using Segmentation Models PyTorch. Through this process, I learned how encoder-decoder architectures can be used for image segmentation tasks and how pretrained encoders can help improve feature extraction and model performance.

  I also gained a deeper understanding of how training configurations influence model performance. To address class imbalance within the damage assessment dataset, we implemented a weighted CrossEntropy loss function that assigns greater importance to underrepresented damage classes. This helps the model learn more effectively across all categories rather than favoring the majority class.

  In addition, we configured the AdamW optimizer and implemented cosine learning rate scheduling. Learning about these techniques helped me understand how models update their parameters during training and how learning rates can be adjusted over time to improve convergence. We also integrated Weights & Biases to track experiments, monitor performance metrics, and create a reproducible training workflow for future model comparisons.

blockers: |
  Several technical challenges arose while implementing the training pipeline. These included resolving import errors, configuring project paths correctly, setting up Weights & Biases authentication, and troubleshooting dataset loading issues. While these problems required additional debugging, they provided valuable experience working with real-world machine learning workflows.

reflection: |
  Today felt like a major milestone in the research project. After spending weeks reviewing research papers, preparing datasets, and designing our methodology, it was exciting to begin building the actual training framework that will support our experiments. I gained a much stronger understanding of how loss functions, optimizers, learning rate schedulers, and experiment tracking tools work together during model training. This experience helped bridge the gap between the theoretical concepts discussed in the literature and their practical implementation in a deep learning pipeline.

what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on monitoring the progress of our baseline U-Net model as it continued training in Google Colab. Since     model training can take several hours, I spent time observing the training process and learning how to interpret the         metrics being produced after each epoch. Rather than simply waiting for the model to finish, I gained a better               understanding of what these values reveal about the model's learning progress.

  One of the biggest concepts I learned today was the purpose of Weights & Biases (W&B) and how it supports machine learning   experiments. W&B automatically records important information such as training and validation loss, learning rate, CPU and    GPU utilization, model configurations, and experiment history. This makes it much easier to compare different training       runs, identify potential issues, and reproduce successful experiments in the future.

  I also learned how to interpret training metrics such as epochs and loss values. Each epoch represents one complete pass     through the training dataset, while the loss function measures how far the model's predictions are from the correct          labels. Watching the loss decrease over time helped me better understand how the model gradually improves its ability to     classify building damage from satellite imagery.

blockers: |
  Since the model training process is computationally intensive, much of today's work involved waiting for training to         complete while carefully monitoring its progress. It required patience to ensure everything continued running correctly      and to verify that no errors interrupted the training process.

reflection: |
  Today's experience helped me realize that training a deep learning model involves much more than writing code.               Understanding training metrics and using tools like Weights & Biases are essential for evaluating model performance and      making informed improvements. As our project progresses, these skills will become increasingly valuable when comparing       different architectures and optimizing our building damage classification models.

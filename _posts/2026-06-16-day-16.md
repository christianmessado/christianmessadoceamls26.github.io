what_i_learned: |
  Today focused on transitioning from dataset preparation into model development and training. One of the main tasks was building and understanding the baseline training      pipeline that will be used to classify building damage within the xBD dataset. We worked through a ResNet50-based U-Net implementation and examined how each component       contributes to the overall machine learning workflow.

  I learned how project paths, datasets, model architectures, loss functions, optimizers, and learning rate schedulers are organized within a training script. We also         incorporated class weights into the CrossEntropyLoss function to address class imbalance within the dataset. This helps ensure that damage categories with fewer examples    receive greater attention during training and are not overlooked by the model.

  Another important topic was experiment tracking using Weights & Biases (W&B). I learned how API keys are used to authenticate training runs and connect them to an online    dashboard where performance metrics such as Overall Accuracy and weighted F1 score can be monitored throughout training. We also discussed how the best-performing model     is automatically saved based on validation performance, which is an important practice in machine learning experimentation.

blockers: |
  A minor challenge involved resolving import and project organization issues related to custom modules such as dataset.py and metrics.py. After reviewing the project         structure and confirming the locations of the required files, I was able to better understand how Python imports modules and how shared project directories are organized    within Google Drive. Once those issues were addressed, the training pipeline became much easier to follow.

reflection: |
  Today was exciting because it felt like a shift from planning and research into actual implementation. Over the past several weeks, our team has spent a significant         amount of time reviewing literature, developing our methodology, and preparing the dataset. Seeing those pieces come together within a working training pipeline helped me   better understand how research ideas are translated into practical machine learning systems.

  I also gained a greater appreciation for the amount of work that happens behind the scenes before a model can even begin training. Understanding how datasets are loaded,    how class imbalance is addressed, how experiments are tracked, and how models are evaluated gave me a much clearer picture of the complete machine learning workflow. I am   looking forward to running experiments, comparing model performance, and seeing how our approach performs on the xBD dataset.
